# 🧠 How to *GET THE IDEA* of Monotonic Stack for Trapping Rain Water

Let’s break this into **4 parts**:

1. What cues the problem gives
2. Why stack is even needed
3. How to identify **which monotonic pattern**
4. How to map it to **NGE / PGE thinking**

---

## 1️⃣ What are the *cues* in the problem statement?

Read the problem again:

> *“How much water can be trapped between bars after raining”*

### Hidden cues 👀

| Cue                       | Why it matters                     |
| ------------------------- | ---------------------------------- |
| “Between bars”            | Needs **left & right boundary**    |
| “After raining”           | Water fills **valleys**, not peaks |
| “Amount depends on shape” | Local comparisons aren’t enough    |
| Array of heights          | Geometry + neighbors               |

🚨 **BIG CUE**

> Water depends on **two sides**, not one.

This already tells you:

* Simple greedy is not enough
* Something must remember **previous bars**

---

## 2️⃣ When does Monotonic Stack naturally appear?

Ask this diagnostic question:

> “Do I need to remember previous elements until a condition is met in the future?”

For this problem:

* A low bar **cannot decide anything by itself**
* It waits until a **right wall appears**
* Until then, it must be **remembered**

👉 This is **exactly when stacks are useful**.

---

## 3️⃣ The KEY observation that leads to STACK

Try to reason without code:

> “When can I say water is trapped?”

Answer:

> When I find a bar that is **taller than something before it**

That’s the *aha moment*.

This sentence alone implies:

```
future element (right wall)
> past element (valley)
```

👉 This is **Next Greater Element style thinking**.

---

## 4️⃣ Why a *Monotonic Decreasing* Stack?

Let’s reason:

* Flat or decreasing heights = downhill
* Water collects only when height **goes up**

So:

> We must detect when the height **increases**

That means:

* We want to react when:

```
height[current] > height[previous]
```

This implies:

* Previous smaller bars must be kept
* Bigger bars invalidate smaller ones

👉 That’s exactly a **monotonic decreasing stack**

---

## 5️⃣ What does the stack REPRESENT here?

This is the MOST IMPORTANT insight.

### Stack does NOT store “water”

### Stack stores **potential LEFT WALLS**

Each element in stack is:

* A bar that might become a **left boundary**
* Waiting for a **right boundary**

---

## 6️⃣ The Exact Moment Water Is Formed

This is the trigger:

```java
height[i] > height[stack.peek()]
```

Translate to English:

> “I have found a bar tall enough to close a valley.”

Now:

* popped bar = valley bottom
* new stack top = left wall
* current bar = right wall

💥 Water is trapped **NOW**, not later.

---

## 7️⃣ Is this Next Greater or Previous Greater?

### Short Answer:

> **It is based on Next Greater Element (NGE)**

But with a twist.

---

### How it matches NGE thinking

| NGE                      | Trapping Rain Water             |
| ------------------------ | ------------------------------- |
| Looking for next greater | Looking for right boundary      |
| Stack waits              | Valley waits                    |
| Pop on greater           | Valley closes                   |
| One pop = one answer     | One pop = one water calculation |

---

### Why it’s NOT Previous Greater

Previous Greater:

* Only looks backward
* No future knowledge

Water trapping:

* Needs **future bar**
* Needs **both sides**

So:
❌ Not PGE
✅ Definitely NGE-style

---

## 8️⃣ Why the Stack is NOT the Final Answer

Another interview-level insight:

In NGE:

* You stop after one pop

In Trapping Rain Water:

* One right wall can resolve **multiple valleys**

So:

```java
while (height[i] > height[stack.peek()])
```

This is **NGE extended to geometry**.

---

## 9️⃣ How to identify monotonic stack problems (CHECKLIST)

When you see a problem, ask:

### ✅ Stack candidate if:

* “Next / previous greater / smaller”
* “First element on left/right”
* “Between two boundaries”
* “Waiting until condition happens”
* “Span / width / area / trapped”

Trapping Rain Water hits **ALL** of these.

---

## 🔟 Final Mental Model (Memorize This)

> **“Whenever a future element resolves a problem created by earlier elements, think monotonic stack.”**

For rain water:

> “A future taller bar resolves earlier valleys.”

---

## 🔁 Summary

* **Cue**: needs left + right boundary
* **Why stack**: valleys must wait
* **Type**: Monotonic **decreasing**
* **Pattern**: Extended **Next Greater Element**
* **Trigger**: height increases
* **Pop meaning**: valley closed

# 🚀 Start

### i = 0 (height = 0)

```text
stack = [0]
```

---

### i = 1 (height = 1)

```text
height[1] > height[0] → 1 > 0 ✅
```

👉 POP!

### 🔥 Pop 0

* stack becomes empty → no left boundary ❌
  👉 no water

Push 1:

```text
stack = [1]
```

---

### i = 2 (height = 0)

```text
0 < 1 → just push
stack = [1,2]
```

---

### i = 3 (height = 2)

```text
2 > height[2]=0 ✅ → POP
```

---

## 🔥 Pop #1

* `top = 2` (bottom)

Now:

```text
left = 1
right = 3
```

### Water:

```text
distance = 3 - 1 - 1 = 1
height = min(1,2) - 0 = 1
water = 1
```

---

### STILL continue (important!!)

```text
2 > height[1]=1 ✅ → POP again
```

---

## 🔥 Pop #2 (MULTIPLE POP!)

* `top = 1`

Now stack becomes empty ❌
👉 stop

Push 3:

```text
stack = [3]
```

---

# 💡 IMPORTANT MOMENT

👉 At i = 3, we popped **TWICE**

Why?
Because:

```text
2 is taller than multiple previous bars
```

👉 So it closes multiple containers

---

### i = 4 (height = 1)

```text

### i = 5 (height = 0)

### i = 6 (height = 1)

distance = 6 - 4 - 1 = 1

```

```

3 > height[6]=1 → POP
```
